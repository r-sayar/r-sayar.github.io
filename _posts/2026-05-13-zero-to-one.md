title: "What does zero mean?"
date: 2026-05-13
---

**0 as a new category, not new atoms.** The components exist; the *thing* didn't. The real test isn't whether you invented new physics — it's whether your thing absorbed other categories and forced a new noun into the language. The smartphone ate the camera, the GPS, the MP3 player, the alarm clock; people stopped calling it a "pocket computer" and started calling it a phone. New behaviors, new word, things it replaced — that's a new category.

**0 as a new capability.** Before this, humans literally could not do X; now they can. Couldn't fly, then could. Couldn't edit specific genes cheaply, then CRISPR. The threshold is functional — what's now possible that wasn't.

**0 as a market with no competitors.** Almost never literally true, and Thiel knows it — he says every monopoly pretends to be in a competitive market and every loser pretends to be a monopoly in some narrow niche. What he actually means is **defensibility**: you've built something no one can credibly copy soon. His four moats — 10x-better proprietary tech, network effects, economies of scale, brand. PayPal had competitors but locked in eBay and got network effects. Not literal 0, but a defensible 1.

**0 as a paradigm shift.** You're not giving a better answer to the existing question — you're making the existing question the wrong one. Copernicus didn't model planetary epicycles better; he said Earth isn't the center, and the question dissolved. Netflix didn't run better video stores than Blockbuster; it made "drive to a store" the wrong frame. Late fees didn't get solved, they got deleted. The test: after this thing exists, does the old way of asking the problem look silly?
